## Competitive Update — Braintrust Loop

**Source:** [Turn production data into better AI with Loop](https://www.braintrust.dev/blog/loop) — Braintrust Blog, November 24, 2025 (not covered in prior digests)

[Braintrust](https://www.braintrust.dev/) launched Loop, an AI agent that automates the most time-intensive parts of eval creation:

- Describes failure modes identified in production data → generates scorer drafts
- Takes plain-language instructions → generates datasets and prompt revisions
- Enables non-technical teammates to participate directly: "Loop enables non-technical teammates to draft scorers by describing failure modes in plain language"
- Braintrust 2026 positioning explicitly calls out PM + QA + domain expert workflows

**Against the goal/story grounding rubric:**
- Does Loop walk a non-engineer from goals → users → stories → what-to-measure? **No.** Loop is a within-Braintrust workflow accelerator. You must already know what failure mode to describe. The upstream discovery problem — *"what should I be measuring?"* — is not addressed.
- Non-technical UX? **Partial → narrowing.** The scorer-from-natural-language pattern is a meaningful reduction in the barrier to participating in evals. This is the most significant non-technical UX push from a funded incumbent.

**Relevance to North Star:** Competitive threat (updated). Loop is the most credible challenge to North Star's non-technical-user claim to date. It doesn't solve the charter-definition problem — it begins where that problem is already solved — but it shrinks the gap between "knowing what to measure" and "having a working eval." North Star's moat is in the discovery phase that runs before Loop is useful.

## Adjacent Lane — Rubric AI (YC W26)

**Source:** [Rubric AI — Y Combinator profile](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/rubric-ai); [Launch coverage — Fondo](https://fondo.com/blog/rubric-ai-launches); [StartupHub overview](https://www.startuphub.ai/ai-news/claudes-corner/2026/claudes-corner-rubric-ai-yc-w2026)

YC Winter 2026 company, launched March 11, 2026 at Demo Day. Founder Pragya Saboo (ex-Oscar Health, ex-Asana). "Reasoning and verification infrastructure for AI agents."

**What it does:** Three components:
1. **Expert-verified reasoning traces** — curated step-by-step problem solutions verified by domain experts (doctors, lawyers, finance professionals), not synthetic data
2. **Runtime guidance** — plugs into agents at inference time to guide tool selection, intermediate step verification, and when to escalate to a human
3. **Training signal generation** — runtime feedback loops back as structured training data

**Target customer:** "Vertical AI companies that have already deployed an agent" in healthcare, legal, finance — engineering-led teams, not PMs.

**Against the goal/story grounding rubric:**
- Does it walk a non-engineer from goals → users → stories → what-to-measure? **No.** Rubric AI is post-deployment runtime infrastructure, not pre-deployment charter definition.
- Non-technical UX? **Unknown.** Customer profile is engineering-led vertical AI companies.

**Name collision risk:** The word "rubric" now means two things in the AI eval space: (1) North Star's eval-criteria framework, and (2) Rubric AI's reasoning infrastructure brand. If North Star's landing page doesn't own the "eval rubric" query in search, Rubric AI may inadvertently capture it.

**Relevance to North Star:** Adjacent lane. Not a direct competitor, but occupies the "expert judgment → AI quality" narrative space and may create search/positioning confusion.

## Methodology Validation

### Anthropic: "Demystifying Evals for AI Agents"

**Source:** [Anthropic Engineering Blog](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/demystifying-evals-for-ai-agents) — January 2026

Anthropic's engineering team published a definitive playbook for agent evaluation drawn from real production deployments (Descript, Bolt AI, others). Key patterns:

- **Three-dimension framing (Descript):** *"Don't break things / Do what I asked / Do it well"* — a user-story-derived rubric remarkably parallel to North Star's CBAR structure. Emerged from what users actually care about, not from abstract metrics.
- **Trajectory vs. outcome metrics:** evaluating the path the agent took, not just final output — maps to North Star's Coverage and Alignment dimensions.
- **LLM-judge calibration:** ground judges against human labels before trusting at scale. Consistent with prior digest findings on judge bias.
- **Multi-turn eval design:** simulated conversation as the evaluation unit, not single prompts. Relevant for conversational AI charters.

**Implication for North Star:** Real product teams independently arrive at rubric-structured, user-story-grounded evals when thinking carefully about their product. The CBAR framework is not a North Star invention — it's a formalization of how good teams naturally reason. Consider whether a plain-language gateway framing ("Don't break things / Do what I asked / Do it well") could precede introducing the acronym on landing pages.

**Relevance:** Methodology validation (agent eval). High fidelity to North Star's dimensional rubric approach.

### O'Reilly Early Release: "Evals for AI Engineers"

**Source:** [O'Reilly Early Release](https://www.oreilly.com/library/view/evals-for-ai/9798341660717/) — Shreya Shankar + Hamel Husain, 2026

The field now has an [O'Reilly book in early release](https://www.oreilly.com/library/view/evals-for-ai/9798341660717/), by the same instructors whose [Maven "AI Evals for Engineers & PMs" course](https://maven.com/parlance-labs/evals) is ranked #1 on the platform. Audience: software engineers, technical PMs, technical leads — not non-technical users. Content covers error analysis, synthetic data generation, LLM-as-judge systems, and production monitoring.

**Relevance:** Methodology maturity signal. When a practice gets an O'Reilly book, it's past the early-adopter phase. The curriculum teaches *how to run* evals but starts by assuming you know *what to* eval. That gap is the North Star pitch: the textbook exists, the tooling exists, but the upstream discovery process — what goals and stories ground the eval — is still missing from the literature.

## Positioning Hints

- **"Loop starts where North Star ends."** When PMs ask why North Star rather than Braintrust's [Loop](https://www.braintrust.dev/blog/loop): Loop generates scorers after you've identified a failure mode. North Star's charter workflow is what you run to know which failure modes matter before you've seen any production data. These are serial steps. Use this framing explicitly.

- **Use Anthropic's three-dimension language as a gateway.** *"Don't break things / Do what I asked / Do it well"* is how real product teams structure evals when they think from user stories. North Star's CBAR maps directly onto this. Open with the plain-language frame before introducing the acronym — it anchors the conversation in product intent, not evaluation jargon.

- **The O'Reilly book signals mainstream arrival.** The curriculum teaches you to run evals but skips the discovery phase. Pitch North Star as the step before Chapter 1: *"Before the book can help you, you need to know what to measure. That's what North Star does."*

- **Own "eval rubric" before [Rubric AI](https://www.ycombinator.com/companies/rubric-ai) does.** Their brand is growing in the agent space. A page or blog post that explicitly defines "eval rubric" in North Star's terms (CBAR, charter, criteria discovery) will protect the term from drift.
