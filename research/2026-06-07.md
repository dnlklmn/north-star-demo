## HIGHEST PRIORITY — EDIT: Training Rubric-Faithful LLM Judges (June 5, 2026)

**Source:** [arXiv:2606.06350 — "EDIT: Evidence-Diagnosed Intervention Training for Rule-Faithful LLM Grading"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06350) (June 5, 2026)

**What it does:** [EDIT](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06350) is a two-phase training framework that addresses a fundamental gap in LLM-as-judge design: rubric grading requires more than accurate score prediction — each judgment must be simultaneously grounded in the rubric (the mark scheme) AND in evidence from the actual output being evaluated. Existing approaches fail because they don't identify *where* in the reasoning chain the grader's beliefs about the correct score start to drift.

The two phases:

1. **EDIT-SFT (Supervised Fine-Tuning):** Locates problematic reasoning steps using two internal model signals — posterior belief over the final mark (does the model's probability distribution over scores shift in the wrong direction?) and input-grounding scores (is the reasoning step actually anchored in the evaluated output or drifting from it?). Only the identified problematic steps are revised with the help of a rubric checklist, leaving correct reasoning intact.

2. **EDIT-RL (Reinforcement Learning):** Calibrates the grader using belief-guided reward shaping: penalises large harmful belief drifts during the reasoning chain, while still allowing helpful exploration. The reward signal is constructed from the trajectory of the model's internal beliefs about the final score, not just the final score itself.

**Why this matters for North Star:**

[EDIT](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06350) is the sixth convergence on rubric-as-central-eval-artifact (joining [Microsoft Universal Verifier](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/blog/universal-verifier/), [Los Alamos arXiv:2605.08061](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.08061), [AdaRubric](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.21362), [MCJudgeBench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.03858), and [RUBAS](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.04051)), but it's the first to directly address the internal grounding mechanism of the scorer.

For North Star specifically:

- North Star's CBAR rubric defines Coverage, Balance, Alignment, and Rot as distinct scoring dimensions. EDIT's finding — that reliable grading requires anchoring each reasoning step in *both* the rubric criterion *and* evidence from the output — validates the per-dimension structure. A judge that evaluates Coverage can only do so reliably if it can locate specific coverage gaps in the output (evidence) relative to the charter's scope definition (rubric).
- The "evidence-diagnosis" framing maps directly to the charter conversation: the charter is the artifact that makes "what counts as evidence for this dimension" explicit before any judge runs. Without a charter, a judge has no anchor for what constitutes relevant evidence per criterion.
- The belief-guided reward shaping in EDIT-RL suggests a concrete approach for training North Star's house judge: use the trajectory of internal confidence about each CBAR dimension, not just the final score, as the training signal.

Prior work ([LongJudgeBench, arXiv:2606.01629](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.01629), June 6 digest) showed rubrics improve but don't fully solve judge reliability. EDIT directly attacks the remaining gap: rubric-faithfulness requires training the judge's reasoning trajectory, not just prompting it with a rubric.

**Relevance to North Star: Methodology validation (highest priority for scorer design).** The CBAR charter is the input to EDIT-style training — it supplies both the rubric dimensions and the evidence types to look for. North Star is upstream of the judge; EDIT is about how to make that judge work correctly.

## Agents' Last Exam (ALE) — 2.6% on Real Industry Tasks (June 5, 2026)

**Source:** [arXiv:2606.05405 — "Agents' Last Exam"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05405) (June 5, 2026); companion site: [agenthle.org](https://agenthle.org/)

**What it does:** [Agents' Last Exam (ALE)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05405) is a benchmark of 960 expert-authored task workflows (1,490 task instances) across 55 digital sub-industries grouped into 13 industry clusters, developed in collaboration with 250+ industry practitioners. Tasks are anchored to the O*NET/SOC 2018 occupational taxonomy — meaning each task is drawn from an actual professional role's documented work, not a synthetic scenario.

Scoring is via deterministic checks and structured rubrics (not open-ended LLM evaluation), assessed at sub-step level within multi-step workflows. The hardest tier — full workflow completion — has a 2.6% average pass rate across mainstream agent harnesses and backbone models. The leaderboard is live at [agenthle.org](https://agenthle.org/).

**Why this matters for North Star:**

ALE validates the charter premise from the *agent performance* side rather than the *evaluation design* side:

- The 2.6% pass rate isn't a model limitation story — it's a *specification gap* story. ALE's tasks are hard to complete because the workflows involve multiple interdependent steps each of which must meet domain-specific quality criteria. Without an explicit rubric anchoring what "good" looks like at each step, agents optimise for task completion signals that don't capture professional quality.
- ALE's use of O*NET taxonomy as the task-structuring layer is the professional-domain equivalent of what North Star's charter does at the product level: define the task envelope (what industry, what role, what workflow) before defining what "done" means. The taxonomy is the meta-charter; each benchmark task is the specific charter.
- The rubric-based scoring approach — not LLM-as-judge for final scores, but structured rubrics with deterministic checks — echoes North Star's CBAR design: dimensional criteria with verifiable scoring rather than holistic judgment.

ALE is a complementary signal to [Asuka-Bench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05920) (June 6 digest): Asuka-Bench showed the performance spread from underspecified code-agent prompts; ALE shows the performance floor when real industry standards are the measurement bar.

**Relevance to North Star: Methodology validation.** ALE demonstrates empirically that agents tested against real professional workflows fail at high rates. The implication: defining the task charter is prerequisite to meaningful agent evaluation, because without it you have no criterion for what "2.6%" is measuring.

## Research Papers

### "Deep Research as Rubric for Reinforcement Learning" — Late Signal (May 31, 2026)

**Source:** [arXiv:2606.01091 — "Deep Research as Rubric for Reinforcement Learning"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.01091) (submitted May 31, 2026; not covered in prior digests)

**What it proposes:** [DR-rubric](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.01091) is a two-stage framework treating rubric construction as an agentic research problem itself. The key observation: open-ended reasoning and long-form generation tasks lack reliable automatic verification signals because identifying what makes a response correct requires discovering and synthesising external domain knowledge first.

- **Stage I (DR-rubric construction):** Iterative multi-turn agentic search elicits domain facts, structural constraints, and failure modes for the task domain
- **Stage II (Distillation):** Synthesises the research output into atomic rubric components, each verifiable and usable as a reward signal in RL training

Results evaluated across 6 benchmarks spanning agentic research and expert reasoning.

**Why this matters for North Star:**

DR-rubric treats the rubric-construction step as requiring active domain knowledge acquisition — which is exactly what North Star's charter conversation does, but with a human domain expert as the knowledge source rather than an agentic search. The parallel is direct:

- DR-rubric Stage I (agentic search for domain facts) = North Star charter conversation (PM or domain expert articulates task goals, users, stories)
- DR-rubric Stage II (distillation into atomic rubric components) = North Star CBAR rubric generation

The paper validates that rubric construction can't be skipped or trivially automated — it requires a deliberate knowledge-elicitation step. This is the sixth institutional convergence on rubric-as-central-artifact and the first to explicitly model the *generation* of rubrics as an iterative elicitation process rather than a one-shot specification.

**Relevance to North Star: Methodology validation (late signal).** Validates the charter-as-rubric-construction-process framing. The knowledge-elicitation structure of DR-rubric mirrors North Star's structured charter conversation.
