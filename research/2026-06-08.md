# Research Digest — 2026-06-08

## TL;DR

- [GEDD (aws-samples/sample-GEDD)](https://github.com/aws-samples/sample-GEDD) is an AWS reference implementation targeting PMs and domain experts with a web annotation workbench — but explicitly "evidence-first" (rubric is downstream of failures, not goals), the inverse of North Star's charter-first approach; same persona, opposite epistemology.
- [Search-Time Contamination in Deep Research Agents](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05241) (arXiv:2606.05241, June 3) shows agents searching the web during eval can inflate benchmark scores by up to 4%; charter-defined custom criteria are structurally contamination-resistant because they're scoped before any public benchmark exists.
- [Generating and Refining Dynamic Evaluation Rubrics for LLM-as-a-Judge](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30568) (arXiv:2605.30568, May 28 — late signal) proposes automated rubric generation without human annotation, seventh convergence on rubric-as-central-eval-artifact; the gap it doesn't close: business goals and user intent are not recoverable from task structure alone.

## ADJACENT LANE — GEDD (AWS sample-GEDD)

**Source:** [GitHub — aws-samples/sample-GEDD: Grounded Eval-Driven Development for AI Agents](https://github.com/aws-samples/sample-GEDD) (MIT-0; updated ~May 18, 2026)

**What it does:** [GEDD](https://github.com/aws-samples/sample-GEDD) is an AWS-native reference implementation (not a commercial product) designed for PMs and domain experts to discover what their AI agents get wrong *before* creating formal evaluation rubrics. Its architecture is explicitly two-persona:

- **Domain Expert Track (steps 1–5):** Load realistic launch-risk scenarios → annotate agent behavior in the expert's own vocabulary (✓/⚠/✗ plus free-text error codes) → map failure patterns to root causes → build release criteria from observed evidence → produce a `session.json` handoff document.
- **ML Engineer Track (step 6):** Convert the `session.json` into SageMaker MLflow experiments, a calibrated LLM judge with rubric weights and hard-fail criteria, and CI/CD regression gates.

The web workbench (`grounded-evals serve`) is the default first experience — non-technical UX is genuine. The integration is AWS-native (Bedrock AgentCore with Claude, SageMaker MLflow).

**The key epistemological claim:** GEDD explicitly states: *"This keeps the rubric downstream of evidence. The expert observes what breaks first, then the judge is built from those observations."*

**Why this matters for North Star:**

GEDD is the sharpest articulation yet of the *alternative* to charter-first that a well-resourced team has shipped. The comparison:

- **GEDD:** Evidence → rubric. You need a running agent and observable failures before you can define quality.
- **North Star:** Goals → users → stories → charter → rubric → dataset → scorers. You define quality before you build the agent, so your first evaluation run isn't your first discovery that you measured the wrong thing.

GEDD's approach has a real strength: it grounds criteria in *actual* failures, not hypothetical ones. Its structural weakness is symmetric: it can only surface failure modes the expert can observe in existing outputs — which means it's blind to the failure modes the agent never attempts because it was never given the goal in the first place. For teams building net-new AI features with no existing outputs, GEDD can't start; North Star can.

The AWS sample format (MIT-0, reference implementation) means this isn't a funded startup competing for the same users — but it does establish a pattern of "PM eval workflow without code" that enterprise teams will encounter as they evaluate tooling.

**Positioning call:** Goal/story grounding: **No** (explicitly evidence-first). Non-technical UX: **Yes** (web workbench). Relevance: **Adjacent lane** (same persona, opposite epistemology).

**Relevance to North Star: Adjacent lane.** Validates the PM + domain expert as the right target persona. Demonstrates demand for non-technical eval tooling. Highlights the clean positioning split: evidence-first (GEDD) vs. goal-first (North Star).

## Research Papers

### Search-Time Contamination in Deep Research Agents (arXiv:2606.05241, June 3, 2026)

**Source:** [arXiv:2606.05241 — "Search-Time Contamination in Deep Research Agents: Measuring Performance Inflation in Public Benchmark Evaluation"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05241) (Yongjie Wang et al., submitted June 3, 2026)

**What it finds:** [Benchmark contamination from training data](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30284) (covered June 1 digest) has a structurally different sibling: *search-time contamination (STC)*, where an agent actively retrieves benchmark metadata, question context, or ground-truth answers from the web during inference itself. The paper defines three contamination severity levels: Benchmark Metadata Leakage → Question-Context Leakage → Explicit Answer Leakage. When evaluating modern deep research agents across six public benchmarks, STC is widespread and inflates performance by up to 4%.

**Why this matters for North Star:**

This is the second contamination validation in eight days ([ProjectionBench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30284) covered training contamination; this covers inference-time search contamination). The convergence strengthens a clean positioning argument:

- Public benchmarks are contaminated at creation (training data) and at evaluation time (web search). Neither form of contamination can affect a charter defined by a specific product team for their specific use case, because that charter's criteria have never appeared on the public web.
- Charter-based evaluation criteria are structurally private: they express business intent that exists only inside the organization. An agent searching the web will never find your organization's Coverage/Balance/Alignment criteria for your specific feature before you've published them.

**Relevance to North Star: Methodology validation.** Strengthens the contamination-resistance argument for custom charter-based evaluation.

### Benchmark Agent — Automated Benchmark Construction (arXiv:2606.06462, June 4, 2026)

**Source:** [arXiv:2606.06462 — "Benchmark Everything Everywhere All at Once"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06462v1) (Xiong et al., CUHK / Huawei, submitted June 4, 2026)

**What it does:** [Benchmark Agent](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06462v1) is a fully autonomous agentic system for benchmark construction. Given a user query (e.g. "build a benchmark for domain X"), it orchestrates the full pipeline: query analysis → subtask design → data annotation → quality control → anti-saturation mechanisms. The system produced 15 representative benchmarks across text, multimodal, and domain-specific reasoning.

**Why this matters for North Star:**

Automated benchmark construction makes the *quantity* of benchmarks trivially scalable. What it cannot do is decide *which* benchmark to build — i.e., it cannot answer "what should we measure and why, given these business goals and user stories?" That remains the charter's job. If anything, Benchmark Agent sharpens the argument: when creating benchmarks is cheap, the scarce resource becomes *knowing what to benchmark*, which is exactly what the charter conversation produces.

**Relevance to North Star: Methodology validation (indirect).** Automated benchmark generation underscores that "what to measure" (charter) is the upstream bottleneck that tooling hasn't touched.

### Late Signal — Generating and Refining Dynamic Evaluation Rubrics for LLM-as-a-Judge (arXiv:2605.30568, May 28, 2026)

**Source:** [arXiv:2605.30568 — "Generating and Refining Dynamic Evaluation Rubrics for LLM-as-a-Judge"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30568) (Zijie Wang & Eduardo Blanco, University of Arizona, posted May 28, 2026)

**What it does:** [This paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30568) is the seventh convergence on rubric-as-central-eval-artifact, and the first to propose fully automated rubric generation without human annotation. Two contributions:

1. **Training-free generation:** Rubrics generated at both dataset-specific granularity (what does this evaluation domain require?) and instance-specific granularity (what does this particular question/task require?). Competitive with human-annotated baselines across four benchmarks.
2. **Iterative fine-tuning:** A rubric generator model fine-tuned via meta-judge reward signals (a judge evaluating the rubric quality) — a 14B fine-tuned model outperforms larger proprietary models at rubric generation.

The key claim: human annotation can be replaced by automatic rubric generation guided by meta-evaluation.

**Why this matters for North Star:**

This is the strongest challenge yet to the "rubric generation requires human elicitation" premise. If rubrics can be auto-generated reliably, why does North Star's charter conversation exist?

The counter-argument, which is durable:

- Auto-generated rubrics capture what the *task* seems to require, inferred from the prompt/question structure and existing responses. They cannot capture what the *business* requires — which goals matter, which user segments are high-priority, what tradeoffs the product has made (e.g. "we accept lower Coverage in exchange for higher Alignment with our brand voice"). None of that is in the task text.
- The meta-judge reward signal optimizes for *internal rubric quality* (consistency, coverage of the task's logical structure). It has no signal for whether the rubric is measuring the right business outcomes.
- North Star's charter conversation is the human-in-the-loop that supplies the signal auto-generation cannot: *what matters here, for these users, in this product context*.

The seven-paper convergence (EDIT, Universal Verifier, Los Alamos, AdaRubric, MCJudgeBench, RUBAS, + this paper) validates that rubric-structured evaluation is the right paradigm. The automated-generation direction strengthens the argument that rubric *format* is solved; rubric *content* aligned to business goals remains the gap.

**Relevance to North Star: Methodology validation (with positioning tension).** Seventh convergence on rubric paradigm; automated generation closes the format gap but leaves the business-goal-alignment gap open — North Star's lane.

## Positioning Hints

- **Name the fault line explicitly.** [GEDD](https://github.com/aws-samples/sample-GEDD) ships "evidence-first" as its documented design principle. This gives North Star a one-sentence contrast: *"Evidence-first is only possible when you already have evidence. Before you build the agent — or when your evidence is biased toward existing behaviour — you need goal-first. That's the charter."*

- **Search-time contamination extends the contamination argument.** Prior digests established training contamination ([ProjectionBench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30284)); [arXiv:2606.05241](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05241) adds inference-time contamination. The combined pitch: *"Public benchmarks are compromised at training time and at evaluation time. Charter-based evaluation criteria are private business intent — they can't be found or gamed."*

- **Automated rubric generation validates the paradigm while opening the pitch.** When [arXiv:2605.30568](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30568) is cited (as it will be in sales conversations), the response: *"Yes — automated tools can generate rubrics that describe the task structure. What they can't generate is why your business cares about Coverage over Alignment, or which user segments define 'good'. That's the charter."*

- **Benchmark Agent frames the scarcity correctly.** [arXiv:2606.06462](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06462v1) makes benchmark creation cheap. Positioning: *"When creating benchmarks is automated, the bottleneck becomes knowing which benchmark to create. North Star is the answer to that question."*
