## Research Cluster — Outcome-Only Eval Is Insufficient (Three Papers)

### 1. Log Analysis Is Necessary for Credible AI Agent Evaluation (arXiv:2605.08545, late signal)

**Source:** [arXiv:2605.08545](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.08545) — Kirgis, Kapoor, Rabanser, et al. (Princeton, UK AISI, Transluce, UC Berkeley); submitted May 8, 2026.

The paper argues that binary pass/fail outcome checks — the standard for agent benchmarks — threaten evaluation credibility in three ways:
1. **Shortcut inflation/deflation:** scores misrepresent capability due to benchmark artifacts
2. **Scaffold failure modes:** benchmark performance fails to predict real-world utility
3. **Hidden dangerous actions:** capability scores can conceal catastrophic intermediate steps

**Key empirical result:** Applied log analysis to [tau-Bench Airline](https://github.com/sierra-research/tau-bench). Pass^5 score was **under-elicited by nearly 50%** — the benchmark was measuring the wrong thing, and nearly half the "successful" capability was invisible to final outcome metrics.

The paper presents a taxonomy of threats to credible evaluation that are only surfaceable via log analysis, and illustrates how recurring failure modes invisible to outcomes become visible when the evaluation is grounded in structured criteria.

**Against the goal/story grounding rubric:** This is not a product — it is empirical evidence for North Star's core thesis. The "deployment failure modes invisible to final outcome metrics" described in the paper are exactly the Rot dimension of CBAR: degradation that passes surface evaluation. The paper's prescription — structured criteria grounding evaluation across the full action trajectory — is what a North Star charter provides upstream of any eval run.

**Relevance to North Star: Methodology validation (strong, institutional provenance).** Co-authored by Princeton, UK AISI, Transluce, and UC Berkeley. The 50% under-elicitation finding is a citable, peer-reviewed number for the cost of skipping structured charter definition. Use in positioning: *"Princeton and AISI researchers showed that outcome-only agent benchmarks under-elicit real capability by up to 50% — because the evaluation criteria weren't grounded in what the agent actually needs to accomplish. A North Star charter defines those criteria before any benchmark is run."*

### 2. Can Agent Benchmarks Support Their Scores? (arXiv:2605.10448, late signal)

**Source:** [arXiv:2605.10448](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.10448) — Gao & Zhou; submitted May 11, 2026.

Agent benchmarks typically report binary outcomes (pass/fail) via outcome checks. This paper proposes a three-step procedure:
1. Apply a **locked checklist** to each completed run
2. Assign one of three **evidence labels**: Evidence Pass, Evidence Fail, or Unknown
3. Report **evidence-supported score bounds** that quantify uncertainty from Unknown cases

The critical insight: when outcome checks rely on surface-level signals, they cannot reliably determine whether a run actually succeeded. The "Unknown" category quantifies how much benchmark confidence depends on unverified assumptions.

**Against the goal/story grounding rubric:** The "Unknown" category is the empirical argument for charter completeness: a charter that doesn't fully specify what a "pass" looks like produces Unknown evidence at evaluation time. Every dimension left unspecified in a CBAR charter maps to an Unknown in this framework.

**Relevance to North Star: Methodology validation.** Provides a framework for explaining why charter completeness matters quantitatively: incomplete charters produce Unknown evidence bands that widen score uncertainty.

### 3. RubricEM: Rubrics as the Shared RL Interface (arXiv:2605.10899, late signal)

**Source:** [arXiv:2605.10899](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.10899) — submitted May 11, 2026.

RubricEM trains deep research agents using rubrics not merely as final-answer evaluators but as "the shared interface that structures policy execution, judge feedback, and agent memory." The framework:
- Conditions planning, evidence gathering, review, and synthesis on self-generated rubrics
- Uses **Stage-Structured GRPO** (stagewise rubric judgments) for denser semantic feedback in long-horizon optimization
- Trains a reflection meta-policy that distills judged trajectories into reusable rubric-grounded guidance

**Results:** RubricEM-8B outperforms comparable open models and approaches proprietary deep-research systems across four long-form research benchmarks.

**Against the goal/story grounding rubric:** RubricEM is a training framework for deep research agents — not an eval platform. But its architectural claim is directly relevant: rubrics are most valuable when they are the **single coherent interface** shared across planning, execution, and evaluation. This is the same claim North Star makes for charters: a charter that is only used for evaluation is less valuable than one that informs what the agent tries to do at every stage.

**Relevance to North Star: Methodology validation (strong).** The academic community is now using rubrics as a central architectural primitive in agent training — not just evaluation post-processing. This strengthens the case that charter-first is not just an evaluation approach but a product development philosophy.

## Research — Multilingual LLM-as-Judge Reliability (arXiv:2605.28710)

**Source:** [arXiv:2605.28710 — Towards Reliable Multilingual LLMs-as-a-Judge: An Empirical Study](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.28710) — Zubiaga et al.; submitted May 27, 2026.

The paper extends LLM-based evaluators to multilingual settings and finds key trade-offs:
- **In-domain (labeled data available):** Fine-tuned smaller models achieve performance comparable to proprietary models
- **Out-of-domain (no labeled data):** Zero-shot evaluation with larger models is more effective
- **Low-resource languages:** LLM-as-judge remains unreliable regardless of approach

**Relevance to North Star: Methodology validation (targeted).** For teams building multilingual AI features, this is an argument for grounding judges in domain-specific rubrics (CBAR) rather than relying on zero-shot LLM judgment. A well-specified charter gives the judge tighter scope per dimension, reducing the surface area where multilingual reliability breaks down. North Star charters may need a "language/locale" field in the Alignment dimension to surface this as a design-time concern.

## Proposed New Matrix Columns

- **Evidence-supported scoring** — Does the tool distinguish between Evidence Pass, Evidence Fail, and Unknown outcomes (per [arXiv:2605.10448](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.10448))? No commercial platform ships this today. Platforms that report only binary pass/fail scores cannot quantify how much their results depend on unverified assumptions.
- **Multilingual judge support** — Does the tool support validated LLM-as-judge evaluation in non-English languages? Per [arXiv:2605.28710](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.28710), this requires either domain-specific fine-tuning or larger zero-shot models — a meaningful engineering distinction.
