## TL;DR

- [**Microsoft RAMPART + Clarity**](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2026/05/20/introducing-rampart-and-clarity-open-source-tools-to-bring-safety-into-agent-development-workflow/) (open-sourced May 20, 2026) pairs a pytest-native CI/CD agentic red-teaming framework ([RAMPART](https://github.com/microsoft/RAMPART)) with [Clarity](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2026/05/20/introducing-rampart-and-clarity-open-source-tools-to-bring-safety-into-agent-development-workflow/), a pre-implementation "structured sounding board" that asks architect/PM/safety-engineer questions before coding begins and saves decisions as structured markdown — the closest any platform has come to the "define before you build" lane, though still developer- and safety-scoped rather than PM-/domain-expert-facing.

Frontier models complete explicit ERP task constraints in only 26.1% of trials, with full optimal solutions in just 17.4%.
- [**iRULER**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.12779) (CHI 2026, late signal — not previously covered): peer-reviewed HCI system that lets non-engineers define evaluation rubrics via guided UI, then validates those rubrics against a "Rubric-of-Rubrics" meta-evaluation layer before applying them — the first peer-reviewed UX proof that user-defined rubric creation can be scaffolded for non-technical users.

## Platform Release — Microsoft RAMPART + Clarity

**Sources:** [Microsoft Security Blog — Introducing RAMPART and Clarity](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2026/05/20/introducing-rampart-and-clarity-open-source-tools-to-bring-safety-into-agent-development-workflow/) (May 20, 2026); [GitHub — microsoft/RAMPART](https://github.com/microsoft/RAMPART)

Microsoft open-sourced two tools on May 20 that together span the "before" and "during" phases of agentic development safety:

**RAMPART (Risk Assessment and Measurement Platform for Agentic Red Teaming):**
- pytest-native CI/CD safety testing framework built on [PyRIT](https://github.com/Azure/PyRIT)
- Supports probabilistic assertions (e.g., "safe in ≥80% of runs") rather than binary pass/fail
- Built-in prompt-injection attack scenarios
- Designed to run in CI pipelines before every deploy

**Clarity:**
- Pre-build AI agent acting as a "structured sounding board" before implementation begins
- Asks architect/PM/safety-engineer questions: What are you building? What are the failure modes? What decisions did you make and why?
- Saves outputs as structured markdown: problem statement, solution rationale, failure analysis, decision log
- Explicitly positioned as "define before you build" tooling for AI features

**Against the goal/story grounding rubric:**
- Guided discovery from goals → users → stories → criteria? **Partial for Clarity.** Clarity asks pre-build design questions and captures structured decisions. It does not walk a PM from business goals through user stories to evaluation dimensions — it is closer to an architecture decision record generator with safety framing. Still: this is the first time a major platform vendor has shipped a "structured sounding board before you write code" tool for AI features.
- Non-technical UX? **No.** Both tools are developer- and security-engineer-facing. Clarity asks architect-style questions; RAMPART is pytest-native.

**Pre-prod / prod path:**
- Pre-prod evals: **Yes** (RAMPART) · Generates synthetic data: **Partial** (RAMPART generates attack scenarios) · BYO dataset: **No** · Prompt versioning: **No** · Prod tracing: **No** · Online scorers: **No** · LLM-as-judge: **Partial** (safety scoring) · Built-in scorers: **Yes** (safety/red-team) · Custom scorers: **Yes** (pytest-native) · Goal/story grounding: **Partial** (Clarity) · Non-technical UX: **No** · Self-hosted: **Yes** · Pricing: **Free OSS**

**Relevance to North Star: Adjacent lane.** Clarity is the most structurally relevant Microsoft tool to North Star yet — it occupies the "structured pre-build design" space, but scoped to safety/failure-mode concerns rather than the full charter question (what good output looks like across all dimensions). The framing gap is sharp: Clarity asks "what could go wrong?" not "what does success look like for your users?" Use in positioning: *"Microsoft's new Clarity tool helps developers think through AI failure modes before they build. North Star helps product teams think through what success looks like before they measure — not just what can break, but what good output means for your specific users and goals."*

## Research — Anchor + ERP-Bench: Domain-Expert Specs as Eval Ground Truth

**Sources:** [arXiv:2605.26321 — Anchor: Mitigating Artifact Drift in Agent Benchmark Generation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.26321) (Ivanov & Rana, submitted May 25, 2026; presented at ACM CAIS 2026 RLEval Workshop, May 26); [ERP-Bench companion site](https://erpbench.ai/)

[Anchor](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.26321) introduces a pipeline for converting domain-expert parametric workflow specifications into coherent agent evaluation benchmarks. The problem it solves — **"artifact drift,"** where instructions, environments, oracles, and verifiers are created by loosely-coupled processes and frequently disagree on what a task requires — is the agent-eval version of the upstream charter problem North Star solves.

**How Anchor works:**
1. A domain expert writes a parametric specification of a business workflow — constraints, preconditions, expected outcomes
2. Anchor generates: natural-language task instructions + environment configuration + solver-certified solution verifier
3. All four artifacts derive from the same spec, eliminating cross-artifact inconsistency

**ERP-Bench:** The resulting benchmark covers 300 long-horizon procurement and manufacturing tasks in [Odoo 19](https://www.odoo.com/) across 29 task patterns (sourced from official Odoo documentation — domain-expert-specified by construction).

**Key results:**
- Frontier models meet explicit ERP task constraints in only **26.1%** of trials
- Reach fully optimal solutions in only **17.4%** of trials
- The gap between constraint completion and optimality is the empirical argument for grounding evaluation criteria in domain-expert specifications rather than benchmark leaderboards

**Against the goal/story grounding rubric:**
- Guided discovery from goals → users → stories → criteria? **Partial.** Anchor converts an expert's constraint spec into solver-certified evaluation — but it assumes the spec already exists. There is no conversational discovery interface, no PM-facing UX. The North Star charter generation phase is precisely the upstream piece Anchor is missing.
- Non-technical UX? **No.** Research pipeline; requires programmatic spec authoring.

**Relevance to North Star: Methodology validation (strong).** Anchor's core claim — that domain-expert specifications should be the single source of truth from which all evaluation artifacts derive — is the empirical argument for charter-first evaluation. The artifact-drift problem (instructions and verifiers that don't agree on what was measured) is exactly what a North Star charter prevents upstream. Use in positioning: *"Researchers at ACM CAIS 2026 showed that AI benchmark failures are often caused by evaluation artifacts that don't agree on what was measured. A North Star charter is the single source of truth that prevents drift — before any artifact is written."*

## Late Signal — iRULER: Rubric Creation for Non-Technical Users (CHI 2026)

**Note:** Submitted February 2026; presented at CHI 2026 (April 13–17). Not previously covered in these digests.

**Sources:** [arXiv:2602.12779 — iRULER: Intelligible Rubric-Based User-Defined LLM Evaluation for Revision](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.12779) (CHI 2026); [ACM Digital Library — iRULER proceedings](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3772318.3790539)

[iRULER](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.12779) is an interactive HCI system with two components:
1. **Rubric Creation app** — non-technical users define custom evaluation rubrics through a guided interface
2. **Writing Revision app** — applies rubrics to LLM-generated text, giving structured, justified, actionable feedback

The novel mechanism: iRULER recursively validates rubrics against a **"Rubric-of-Rubrics"** meta-evaluation layer before applying them. The meta-rubric checks whether a user-created rubric is well-specified, non-overlapping, actionable, and complete — and suggests improvements if not.

**Controlled study results:**
- iRULER most improved LLM-judged review scores compared to static rubric or plain-text LLM feedback
- Rated most helpful and best-aligned by study participants
- The two-role design (separate rubric creation and revision apps) enables stakeholder-split workflows: a domain expert creates the rubric, a different user applies it — structurally similar to North Star's charter-to-dataset handoff

**Against the goal/story grounding rubric:**
- Guided discovery from goals → users → stories → criteria? **No.** iRULER is scoped to writing revision (essays, documents), not AI product/feature evaluation. No business-goals-to-stories pipeline.
- Non-technical UX? **Partial.** Rubric creation UI is designed for non-technical users; study was in academic/educational contexts (essay review), not AI product evaluation.

**Relevance to North Star: Methodology validation (direct, peer-reviewed).** iRULER provides three things:
1. **Peer-reviewed UX validation** that non-engineers can define evaluation criteria via guided interfaces — the same UX hypothesis underlying North Star's charter generation flow.
2. **The Rubric-of-Rubrics pattern** — a meta-validation layer that should be applied to CBAR charters before dataset generation begins. A charter with a non-measurable Coverage dimension or an overlapping Balance/Alignment definition should trigger a charter revision loop before dataset synthesis.
3. **Study data** showing rubric-based LLM feedback is more helpful than either static rubrics or unstructured LLM feedback — the empirical case for CBAR's structured per-dimension approach over holistic quality scoring.

## Positioning Hints

- **Clarity vs. North Star: "what can break" vs. "what good looks like."** [Microsoft Clarity](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2026/05/20/introducing-rampart-and-clarity-open-source-tools-to-bring-safety-into-agent-development-workflow/) frames pre-build design as failure-mode analysis. North Star frames it as success-criteria definition. These are complementary but distinct: *"Microsoft's Clarity helps engineers think through failure modes before writing code. North Star helps product teams think through what success looks like for users and goals — not just what can break, but what good means."*

- **Anchor names the downstream cost of skipping a charter.** [arXiv:2605.26321](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.26321) quantifies "artifact drift" — the cost when your evaluation instructions, environments, and verifiers don't agree on what they're measuring. Use the term and cite the evidence: *"Researchers call it 'artifact drift.' Your test instructions and your scoring verifier measuring different things. A North Star charter is the single source of truth that prevents it — before any artifact is written."*

- **iRULER's Rubric-of-Rubrics is a feature, not just a research concept.** [iRULER](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.12779) found that rubrics created by non-experts need meta-validation before they can be trusted. This maps to a concrete North Star step: charter validation against a meta-rubric (measurable? non-overlapping? actionable?) before dataset generation begins. *"Your charter gets validated against a quality rubric before we generate any data — because a poorly-specified Coverage dimension produces a dataset that measures the wrong thing."*

- **The "pre-build design" lane is officially contested — but only from the safety angle.** [Clarity](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2026/05/20/introducing-rampart-and-clarity-open-source-tools-to-bring-safety-into-agent-development-workflow/) occupies "define before you build" for safety engineers. North Star occupies it for product/quality thinking. Use this to sharpen the category definition: North Star is not a safety tool — it is the quality definition layer that safety tools presuppose has already been done.

## Proposed New Matrix Columns

- **Pre-build design phase** — Does the tool have a structured phase *before* evaluation begins, in which the team defines what to measure, what failure modes to address, or what success criteria apply? Distinct from "pre-prod evals" (which measures against already-defined criteria) — this is the upstream definition step. [Clarity](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2026/05/20/introducing-rampart-and-clarity-open-source-tools-to-bring-safety-into-agent-development-workflow/) is the first major platform entry (Partial, safety-scoped); North Star is the full answer. The existing "Goal/story grounding" column partially covers this but conflates "can derive criteria from goals" with "has a structured pre-eval design phase."
- **Meta-rubric validation** — Does the tool validate the quality of evaluation criteria/rubrics before running evals? [iRULER's](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.12779) Rubric-of-Rubrics is the peer-reviewed proof-of-concept; no commercial platform ships this today. A "Yes" here would mean the platform checks that your dimensions are measurable, non-overlapping, and actionable before generating datasets or running scorers.
